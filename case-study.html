Ripple

How do you design a social tool for people whose energy — and availability changes moment to moment?

Role
Lead UX Designer, Project Manager

Team

Tools

01 Overview
Context
A 73-hour hackathon exploring our senses beyond the typical 5. Ripple is a mobile app that helps people with chronic illness or fluctuating energy visualize their social energy and communicate their availability without over-explaining or canceling last-minute.
My Role
Led research research, synthesis, defined the core interaction model, designed the aura visualization system, and built the low-fidelity prototype in Figma and FigBuild.
Methods
Rapid user interviews · Affinity mapping · Competitive analysis · Low-fidelity prototyping · Usability walkthroughs · Accessibility review
Outcome
Delivered a functional prototype and pitch deck in 48 hours. The aura system was cited by judges as a novel approach to ambient social communication.
02 The Problem

People with chronic illness, POTS, or fluctuating energy conditions face a specific social problem: their availability changes hour to hour, and existing tools offer no way to communicate that nuance. Saying "I'm free Saturday" means something different when your energy is unpredictable.

The current options — text messages, calendar blocks, social media — all require people to either over-explain their condition or simply cancel at the last minute. Both options create guilt, erode relationships, and make social connection feel costly rather than restorative.

Core Problem Statement

How might we help people with fluctuating energy communicate their social availability authentically — without requiring them to explain their condition every time?

03 Research & Discovery

Given the 48-hour constraint, we prioritized rapid qualitative research over breadth. I conducted 7 short interviews with people who identified as living with chronic illness, POTS, or fluctuating energy conditions — recruited through a personal health advocacy network.

I used FigJam to run a live affinity mapping session with the team immediately after interviews, clustering findings into four categories: Energy Unpredictability, Social Pressure, Communication Barriers, and Coping Strategies.

The clearest pattern: users weren't looking for a scheduling tool. They wanted a way to signal their energy state — like a status light — without having to write an explanation every time. Several described existing workarounds: custom emoji systems, group chats with coded language, avoiding plans altogether.

Accessibility surfaced as a core requirement, not an afterthought. Most participants used screen readers or voice control at low-energy moments — meaning any ambient visualization needed a text-based alternative.

Participant 3 · Interview
"I just stop making plans. It's easier than explaining why I might cancel at 6pm after saying yes at 10am."
Avoidance as a coping strategy — the burden of explanation is high enough that opting out feels easier than communicating.
Participant 6 · Interview
"My friends know that a green heart emoji means I'm okay today and a purple one means I need to take it easy. We just made that up."
Users are already designing ambient energy signals informally — Ripple formalizes a behavior that already exists in the wild.
Key Research Finding

Users don't need a better calendar. They need a way to say "I'm a maybe" without writing a paragraph — and they've been building workarounds for this for years.

The Challenge
An animated aura visualization is beautiful on screen but completely inaccessible for screen reader users — the same population Ripple is built for.
The Decision
We designed the aura as a visual layer over a text-based energy state system. The text states — "full energy," "taking it easy," "offline today" — are the primary data. The aura is the enhancement.
Accessibility-first meant the visual system had to earn its place on top of a working text foundation.
05 Final Design

The final Ripple prototype centers on an ambient home screen — a soft animated aura that reflects the user's current energy state, set by them at any time. From this state, social invites can be sent or received with full context: not "are you free Saturday?" but "I'm at half energy Saturday — want to do something low-key?"

The invitation system is built around energy-matched activity suggestions, reducing the cognitive load of planning while keeping social connection accessible at every energy level.

06 Outcomes
Judges cited the aura system as the most novel interaction model in the hackathon — the only entry addressing ambient social communication for chronic illness.
All 3 participants in usability walkthroughs understood the energy state system without instruction — the visual metaphor tested as immediately intuitive.
Accessibility-first architecture validated: text-based energy states tested successfully with a screen reader before any visual layer was applied.
The project is being developed further as a Spring 2025 portfolio piece with extended research and higher-fidelity prototyping.
What this taught me

Designing for a community you're personally part of is both an advantage and a risk. I had to work harder to separate my own assumptions from what participants actually said — and that discipline made the research stronger.

What Worked
Starting with behavioral observation instead of feature ideas. The aura visualization emerged directly from how users were already signaling energy — we formalized an existing behavior rather than inventing a new one.
What I'd Change
With more time I'd have tested the energy scale more rigorously — 3 states vs 5 vs a continuous slider. The decision to use 3 was informed but not validated with enough participants.
Next Steps
Extend the research to 15+ participants. Prototype the notification system — how does Ripple communicate a friend's energy change without creating anxiety? Build a high-fidelity version for portfolio presentation.

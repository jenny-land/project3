01 Overview
Context
Bondfire is a native Slack application that transforms team bonding from a scheduled obligation into spontaneous moments of authentic connection. Four activity types — Choose Your Own Adventure, Name That Tune, Personal Trivia Cards, and Kudos — live directly inside Slack, with no context-switching required.
My Role
End-to-end UX — from research through beta-ready MVP. Led all user research, defined design principles, designed all four activity flows, the analytics dashboard, and the full component library. Collaborated directly with the engineering co-founder on Slack Block Kit constraints and technical feasibility throughout.
Methods
Ethnographic observation · Semi-structured interviews (15 team leaders) · Survey research (50+ remote workers) · Competitive analysis (12 tools) · 12 moderated usability sessions · 5 unmoderated remote tests · A/B testing · Heat map analysis · Analytics dashboard design
Outcome
Beta-ready MVP with 74% participation rate, 86% completion rate, and NPS of 8.3 from employees / 8.7 from team leaders. 4CD Business Pitch Finalist. Active product continuing to evolve based on user feedback and market needs.
02 The Problem

Remote work created a specific kind of loneliness — the kind that exists alongside constant communication. You're in meetings all day, on Slack all day, and still don't know anything real about the people you work with.

Existing team-building solutions made this worse in three ways: they required leaving the workflow entirely (average 45–60 minutes to organize an activity), they were built around competition in industries already saturated with it (68% of surveyed employees reported "competition fatigue"), and they gave leadership nothing measurable — no way to prove engagement ROI or understand what was actually happening with team health.

The deeper problem was the design assumption underneath all of it: that team bonding tools are for extroverts. People who enjoy being put on the spot, who thrive in group settings, who don't find "mandatory fun" exhausting. Alex — the introverted engineer who just wants to know his teammates without a Zoom call — had no tool built for him.

Core Design Challenge

How might we help distributed teams build authentic connections inside their existing workflow — without disrupting deep work, rewarding only extroverts, or giving leadership nothing to measure?

03 Research & Discovery

Research began with ethnographic observation of distributed teams using Slack — watching how people actually communicated, what they engaged with, and what they scrolled past. This grounded the work before a single interview question was written.

I conducted 15 semi-structured interviews with team leaders and managers, and surveyed 50+ remote workers about connection and engagement. Competitive analysis covered 12 existing team engagement tools — looking not just at features but at the UX mistakes each one made and why participation dropped off.

Three personas emerged that were not interchangeable: Sarah (People Ops Director, needs measurable ROI and culture data), Marcus (Engineering Team Lead, needs his team to trust each other fast), and Alex (Software Engineer, wants organic connection without forced participation). The key research insight was that most tools designed for Sarah and Marcus at the expense of Alex — and Alex's disengagement undermined everything Sarah and Marcus were trying to achieve.

The design had to work for all three simultaneously. That constraint became the creative engine for every decision that followed.

Sarah · People Ops Director · Primary Persona
"I know team bonding is important, but I need to prove it's working."
Leadership needed evidence, not entertainment. The analytics dashboard wasn't a nice-to-have — it was the feature that made budget justification possible and differentiated Bondfire from every competitor.
Alex · Software Engineer · Tertiary Persona
"I want to connect with my team, but I don't want another mandatory meeting."
Alex was the hardest user to design for and the most important. If the introverted deep-work engineer opts out, participation rates collapse and the product fails for everyone. Designing for Alex first made the whole system stronger.
Key Research Finding

Existing tools designed for the most willing participants and called it good. The dropout came from everyone else. Bondfire had to earn participation from the people least inclined to give it — or the engagement numbers would never be real.

04 Process
1
Research & Principles
Ethnographic observation, 15 interviews, 50+ surveys, competitive analysis of 12 tools. Established 4 design principles before wireframing: Slack-Native First, Participation Over Performance, Respect for Real Work, Strategic Intelligence.
2
Architecture & Flows
Designed the full information architecture and user flows across all four activity types. Sketched 20+ variations of game initiation flows, tested thread-based vs. modal-based gameplay, and explored notification patterns for minimal disruption.
3
Prototype & Test
12 moderated usability sessions, 5 unmoderated remote tests, A/B testing on notification wording (3 variants), heat map analysis of the analytics dashboard. Each testing round produced specific iteration decisions — not just general feedback.
4
Iterate to Beta
Four major iteration rounds on: game launch flow (90s to 15s time-to-launch), points visibility (34% increase in mid-game engagement), notification strategy (opt-out rate from 23% to 4%), and analytics privacy controls (91% opt-in after redesign).

The Challenge
The original game launch flow had 6 configuration options in a multi-step modal — team leader had to manually select participants, choose settings, and preview before launching. Testing showed it felt overwhelming, caused accidental exclusions, and averaged 90 seconds from intent to launch. Leaders who found it cumbersome simply didn't start games.
The Decision
One-step launch with smart defaults. Auto-includes all channel members. Advanced options collapsed but accessible. Immediate launch with an edit-in-progress option for leaders who want control without needing it upfront. 87% of users now launch with default settings — validation that the right defaults are better than more options.
Time-to-launch reduced from 90 seconds to 15 seconds. Accidental exclusions: 0. The best interface is one that gets out of the way.
05 Final Design

The final MVP covers four distinct activity types designed for different team sizes, energy levels, and personality types — ensuring no single working style is left out. All gameplay happens in Slack threads, keeping main channels clean. Points are earned for participation, not just winning. Leader controls (pause, skip, end) are always visible, respecting that work comes first.

The analytics dashboard was designed from the start as a first-class feature, not an afterthought. It gives People Ops teams a composite Team Health Score, participation trends, employee preference data captured through gameplay, and actionable insights — including engagement risk indicators and automated recommendations. 94% of leaders access it weekly, averaging 12 minutes per session.

06 Outcomes
4CD Business Pitch Finalist — research insights and product strategy presented to judges and stakeholders in a competitive pitch environment. Bondfire is an active product preparing for public launch.
74% participation rate (target: 70%+) · 86% completion rate (target: 80%+) · 81% weekly return rate (target: 75%+). All three core engagement metrics exceeded targets in beta testing.
NPS 8.3 from employees / 8.7 from team leaders — both above target of 7+. The dual NPS tracks both user groups separately, confirming the design worked for employees and leadership simultaneously.
Analytics privacy redesign achieved 91% opt-in rate after adding explicit consent, aggregated-only data, and a user-facing privacy dashboard. Zero privacy complaints in beta. Legal and compliance approval significantly easier than with the original design.
Notification strategy redesign reduced opt-out rate from 23% to 4% and increased join rate from 62% to 78% — by moving from DM-based to channel-based notifications with smart timing that respects focus mode and DND settings.
92% of beta testers felt "comfortable participating" — the clearest validation of the "participation over performance" principle. Everyone earns points. Winners get recognition. Nobody gets left out.
What this taught me

Designing for three stakeholders with genuinely different goals forces clarity. Every time I had to make a decision, I had to answer for Alex, Marcus, and Sarah simultaneously. That constraint produced better design than any single persona would have.

What Worked
"Participation over performance" as a design principle, not just a value statement. It resolved dozens of decisions — points distribution, game mechanics, notification wording — without requiring a separate debate each time. Principles that do real work are worth more than principles that sound good.
What I'd Change
I should have engaged the buyer persona (Sarah, HR/leadership) earlier in the process. The analytics feature came later than it should have — it became clear mid-project that it was the feature that justified the budget conversation, not a nice addition. The research was there. The prioritization came too late.
Next Steps
AI-powered personalization — game recommendations based on team patterns, adaptive difficulty, optimal timing suggestions. Expand to Microsoft Teams. Build a custom game creator for enterprise accounts. Deepen the analytics with predictive engagement risk indicators and benchmarking against similar companies.

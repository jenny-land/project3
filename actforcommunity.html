Act for Community

A student-led nonprofit in Uzbekistan had a strong community presence and no website to show for it. Two months, one designer, and a team learning HTML for the first time.

01 Overview
Context
Act for Community is a high school–led organization in Uzbekistan that organizes fundraising events and volunteers with local groups. They had an active Instagram presence but no centralized platform to share their work with college recruiters or expand outreach. Through Code the Change, our team partnered with them to design and build their first website.
My Role
I was the sole designer on a team of primarily computer science students. My role started as UI design and expanded considerably — into client communication, front-end template development, Bootstrap implementation, and hands-on support to help the team cross the finish line.
Methods
Client alignment interviews · Requirement gathering · UI design · HTML/CSS template development · Bootstrap implementation · Front-end walkthroughs · Implementation support & troubleshooting
Outcome
Complete website design and front-end implementation delivered within the project timeline. Client approved the design and expressed excitement about having a dedicated site to share with recruiters and community partners.
02 The Problem

Act for Community had real impact — events organized, volunteer hours logged, partnerships with local organizations. But none of that was visible anywhere beyond Instagram. For a student-led group trying to establish credibility with college recruiters and community partners, a social-only presence was a genuine limitation.

The ask was relatively contained: a simple website that presented their work clearly and helped them look like the legitimate organization they already were. But "simple" is relative. The project quickly surfaced a second, less visible problem — the team tasked with building the site had little to no front-end experience, which meant the design had to be shaped with implementation reality in mind from the start.

During early conversations with the client representative, I noticed an opportunity that wasn't in the original brief. The organization wasn't just trying to document their past — they wanted to grow. I suggested including a contact pathway so external organizations could reach out for volunteer support. That shifted the site from a static showcase into something with a functional purpose.

The core design challenge became balancing three things at once: what the client needed, what the team could realistically build, and what would actually serve the organization's goals beyond this project.

Core Design Challenge

How might we design a credible, functional website for a student-led nonprofit — working within fixed client content, a compressed timeline, and a development team learning front-end for the first time?

03 Research & Discovery

Research here looked different than a typical discovery phase. There was no time for extensive user testing, and the client content was largely fixed — pages, copy, images, and brand colors were all provided. What I could do was ask better questions upfront and make sure the design decisions that followed were actually grounded in something.

I met with the client representative to understand the required site structure, visual preferences, and — most importantly — who the primary audience was. That conversation made it clear the site wasn't just for the community; it was a credential. College recruiters would be looking at it. That reframed every hierarchy and content decision that came after.

I also spent time early on understanding the team's technical capabilities. Most of the developers had never written HTML or CSS before. That wasn't a complaint — it was a design constraint. A layout that looked good in Figma but required complex CSS would stall in development.

Knowing this shaped the research questions I was asking: not just "what does the client want?" but "what can we actually deliver, and what does that mean for the design decisions I make today?" That kind of constraint-aware thinking ended up being one of the more valuable skills I practiced on this project.

Key Discovery

The most important research on this project wasn't about the end user — it was about the team delivering the product. Understanding implementation constraints early is its own form of user research. It shapes what you design just as much as knowing your audience.

04 Process
1
Client Alignment
Met with the client representative to clarify required pages, visual preferences, brand colors, and intended audience. Advocated internally for time to understand requirements before designing — an early lesson in why rushing to execution before alignment is a risk, not efficiency.
2
Design for Reality
With team capability in mind, I intentionally kept layouts structured and predictable, minimized complex interactions, and prioritized clear visual hierarchy over creative complexity. The goal wasn't to reduce quality — it was to ensure the design could actually be built by the team we had.
3
Rapid Execution
Completed the full design within approximately one week to give maximum time for development. Used the client's color palette in a more cohesive way, established clear typographic hierarchy, and added a contact pathway that hadn't been in the original brief but served the organization's actual goals.
4
Front-End Bridge
When development stalled, I created an HTML/CSS starter template, introduced Bootstrap to simplify layout and responsiveness, and led working sessions to walk the team through front-end basics. Stepped into the gap not because it was my job — but because the project needed it and I could help.

The Challenge
Midway through the project, development progress had stalled. The team was unfamiliar with HTML and CSS, and the gap between the design and a working implementation was widening. The timeline didn't have room to wait for the team to ramp up independently.
The Decision
I built an HTML/CSS starter template and introduced Bootstrap to give the team a structured foundation they could work from. Then I ran working sessions — walking through layout logic, troubleshooting issues in real time, and helping people understand what they were building rather than just giving them answers.
Bridging design and development wasn't in the original scope. But knowing when to step into a gap — and how to do it without taking over — is its own kind of design skill.
05 Final Design

The final site was clean, structured, and purposeful. It used the client's brand colors in a more cohesive way than their existing materials, established clear hierarchy across pages, and communicated the organization's work in a way that would read as credible to an outside audience — especially the college recruiters they were trying to reach.

The contact pathway I introduced gave the site a functional purpose beyond documentation. External organizations could now reach out directly to collaborate or request volunteer support — something the original brief hadn't included but that served the organization's actual goals.

Every layout decision was made with the development team in mind. Structured grids over complex compositions. Minimal motion. Bootstrap-friendly components. The design looked intentional because it was — and part of that intention was making sure it could be built.

06 Outcomes
Full website delivered within the project timeline — complete design and front-end implementation across all required pages, meeting the client's stated requirements for content, brand colors, and structure.
Client approval and positive response — the client expressed genuine excitement about having a dedicated website to share with college recruiters and community partners. That was the goal from the start.
Contact pathway added to the original scope — a feature I proposed during early client conversations that expanded the site's function from static showcase to active outreach tool.
Development team supported through completion — by stepping in with templates, Bootstrap guidance, and working sessions, I helped a team with no front-end experience deliver a functional, client-approved build.
First real-world client project as a designer — navigated ambiguity, shifting team dynamics, and real constraints without a safety net. Everything that went well and everything that was hard was a genuine learning.
What this taught me

This was my first experience working on a product with a separate development team, and it taught me how quickly a project can shift when execution risks aren't caught early. Design doesn't end at handoff — sometimes it extends into making sure the handoff actually works.

What Worked
Designing with implementation constraints in mind from the start. Keeping layouts simple and structured wasn't a limitation — it was a strategic call that made delivery possible. When I introduced Bootstrap and a starter template, the team had something concrete to build from rather than starting from nothing.
What I'd Change
I would surface technical alignment conversations earlier — ideally in the first week, before design began. Understanding the team's capabilities upfront would have shaped the design approach from the start, rather than requiring mid-project pivots. Earlier alignment means fewer surprises for everyone.
Next Steps
Build stronger handoff processes between design and development in future team projects. Expand usability validation beyond internal review. Continue developing front-end skills to reduce the gap between what I design and what I can help build — that bridge has real value on small teams.

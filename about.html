01 Introduction

I'm a UX designer and researcher focused on building interaction systems that feel clear, human, and grounded in how people actually behave — not how we assume they do.

My approach starts with observation. Before I sketch a single screen, I want to understand what users are really trying to accomplish, where they stall, and what they're not saying out loud. That attentiveness — to behavior, to language, to the gap between what people say and what they do — is something I developed long before I knew what UX was.

I'm currently based in the Bay Area, completing my Interaction Design certificate at Diablo Valley College, and actively seeking full-time UX researcher and product designer roles at product-focused teams.

"The best interfaces disappear. The best research finds what people couldn't say themselves."
Currently based in the Bay Area. Open to full-time roles, internships, and contract work in UX research and product design — especially at teams building accessible, thoughtful digital products.
02 How I Think

I approach every project by asking four questions before I open Figma.

01
Who can't use this?
Accessibility isn't a checklist — it's the first constraint. Designing for the edges makes the whole system better.
02
What are people not saying?
Users describe symptoms, not causes. My job is to hear the frustration underneath the feature request.
03
Where does trust break down?
Unclear feedback, unpredictable states, and missing context erode confidence. I look for where a system stops communicating.
04
What happens at the edges?
Empty states, errors, and transitions are where experience is shaped — or lost. I design for the full range, not just the happy path.

Good design isn't just visual — it's the result of asking the right questions before picking up a tool.

03 Background

For nine years I worked in early childhood education — an environment that demands extraordinary attentiveness. Children can't always tell you what they need. They show you through behavior, frustration, and the moments they disengage.

That experience built my core UX muscle: the ability to observe without judgment, to separate what someone says from what they mean, and to design for people who cannot fully articulate their own needs. It's the same skill set, applied to a different canvas.

"Watching someone understand something for the first time never gets old — whether that's a four-year-old or a first-time user."

04 What I'm Looking For

I'm drawn to product teams working on problems that actually affect people's daily lives — healthcare tools, accessibility-focused products, civic tech, and collaborative platforms where clear design genuinely changes outcomes.

I thrive in environments that treat research and design as equals — where "we should talk to users before we build" is the default, not the exception. I work best alongside developers, where I can understand constraints early and help make research findings actionable.

CalCal
Calendar App

Calendar apps were built for one time zone. What happens when your entire life — personal and professional — crosses several?

01 Overview
Context
A full end-to-end UX project completed in Interaction Design for Web and Mobile Platforms at Diablo Valley College. CalCal is a calendar app concept designed for international professionals and students who need smarter timezone handling, flexible reminders, and seamless calendar syncing.
My Contributions
POV statements · HMW questions (primary adopted by team) · Task flows · User flow design · Participant recruiting · Moderated usability session facilitation · Report writing · Introduction & conclusion
Methods
Semi-structured interviews · Survey research · Affinity mapping · Competitive analysis · Persona development · Task flow & user flow design · Figma prototyping · Maze unmoderated testing · Moderated in-person usability sessions
Outcome
Interactive Figma prototype covering 3 core task flows, tested with 15 remote and local participants via Maze. Usability data confirmed where the design succeeded and where it created friction — with specific, actionable recommendations for each.
02 The Problem

Mainstream calendar apps are built around the assumption that the user lives and works in a single timezone. The moment your team, your family, or your social life crosses a border, that assumption breaks — and users are left manually calculating time differences, relying on third-party tools, and still making scheduling errors.

Three distinct problem areas emerged from initial research: managing personal events across timezones (birthday reminders that fire at the wrong time), coordinating business meetings with international colleagues without conflict detection, and reminder reliability for high-stakes events where a single notification isn't enough.

The problem isn't just professional — it's relational. Missing a friend's birthday across timezones because the app didn't account for the difference isn't a workflow issue. It's a human one.

Primary HMW — Authored by Jennifer, Adopted by Team

How might we create a calendar app that seamlessly adapts to multiple time zones and integrates with existing tools to help busy professionals manage international projects without manual time adjustments — reducing workflow interruptions and minimizing miscommunication?

03 Research & Discovery

The team conducted semi-structured interviews and surveys with 9 participants — a mix of college students and working professionals between ages 18 and 54. Research targeted two core groups: people managing complex international schedules and students balancing unpredictable, multi-context commitments.

I led participant recruiting and facilitated moderated in-person sessions alongside Maze's unmoderated remote testing. The combination gave us both the depth of observed behavior and the breadth of quantitative task metrics — neither alone would have been sufficient.

Findings were synthesized through affinity mapping into four themes: Reminder Preferences, Timezone Management, Integration Needs, and Complex Schedules. Secondary research validated our primary findings — a published case study by designer Himanshu Singh documented nearly identical friction points with Google Calendar's timezone handling, confirming we weren't designing for edge cases.

Competitive analysis covered Google Calendar (55.6% of participants' primary tool), Apple Calendar, Re:Catch, and Momentum. None offered native timezone conflict detection. Users were solving the problem themselves — with third-party tools, manual calculation, and workarounds.

Participant Feedback · Usability Test
"Overall layout is confusing. What does this app solve? Why is it better than a regular calendar app?"
Multiple participants couldn't articulate CalCal's value proposition over Google Calendar — revealing a gap between the features we designed and how we surfaced their purpose. The app needed onboarding before the home screen.
Participant Feedback · Create Event Flow
"Too many questions to create an event. Looked cluttered and boring — it just felt like work."
Task completion rates alone don't capture experience quality. Users completed the Create Event flow at 71.4% — but described it as frustrating. That gap between completion and satisfaction is where retention is lost.
Key Research Finding

Task success rates are an incomplete quality signal. CalCal's participants completed flows at acceptable rates while simultaneously describing the experience as cluttered and frustrating — a retention risk that the numbers alone would never have revealed.

04 Process
1
Discovery & Framing
Interviews, surveys, competitive analysis, and secondary research. Team members developed independent POV statements before converging — my HMW formulation was adopted verbatim as the team's primary framing.
2
Flows & Architecture
I designed all three task flows and user flows in Figma: Create Event (with timezone conflict detection branch), Sync Calendar, and Set Time Zone. These flows became the direct basis for all three Maze usability test tasks.
3
Wireframe & Prototype
Team built low-fidelity wireframes across all core screens, progressing to an interactive Figma prototype. I contributed to IA and layout structure. The prototype covered all three flows including the conflict detection decision branch.
4
Test & Synthesize
Unmoderated Maze testing with 15 remote participants plus moderated local sessions. I led recruiting and facilitated in-person sessions. First Maze test had to be rebuilt after prototype was modified post-launch — locked copies from that point forward.
Task flows & user flows — Create Event, Sync Calendar, Set Time Zone

Three task flows mapped in Figma. The Create Event flow includes a timezone conflict detection branch — the most complex path and the hardest usability test task.

The Challenge
The Create Event form tried to do everything on one screen: event name, all-day toggle, start and end times with timezone display, recurrence, attendees, reminders, and conflict warnings. Usability testing showed the highest friction of any flow — 71.4% success, 71.1% misclick rate, and qualitative feedback describing it as "cluttered" and "just work."
The Recommendation
Split the form into progressive steps: Basic Info → Reminders → Attendees → Confirm. The pattern mirrors Google Calendar's stepped creation, reduces cognitive load, and creates natural checkpoints where the conflict detection warning has room to live without overwhelming the primary task.
The most valuable feature — timezone conflict detection — was also the hardest to find. Good research reveals not just what's broken, but what's buried.
05 Final Design

The prototype addressed all three research-validated problem areas: a timezone settings flow that lets users toggle between device timezone and a searchable manual zone, a reminders overlay with stacked notifications and a persistent reminder option, and a calendar sync screen supporting OAuth connection to external services.

The highest-performing screen in usability testing was the reminders overlay — 92.9% task success, 7.1% drop-off, intuitive enough that participants rarely misclicked. The lowest was the Find Better Time feature — 0 direct successes, entirely reliant on indirect paths. The gap between those two numbers is itself a research finding: discoverability is a design problem, not a user problem.

06 Outcomes & Testing Results
Reminders overlay: 92.9% task success — the best-performing flow in the entire prototype. 7.1% drop-off, 25% misclick rate. Participants described it as intuitive and well-designed without prompting.
My HMW question adopted verbatim by the full team as the primary framing for the project. Written during the diverge-then-converge POV session, it survived peer review unchanged — and became the compass for every design decision that followed.
Settings-based flows (Set Time Zone, Sync Calendar) both achieved 86.7% task success — above target. Learnable paths with acceptable misclick rates, confirming the settings architecture was sound even if the entry point wasn't obvious.
Find Better Time: 0 direct successes across all test participants. All completions were indirect. A critical insight — the most valuable feature was entirely undiscoverable, and would have been invisible in the shipped product without this data.
Led recruiting and facilitation for both unmoderated Maze sessions (15 remote participants) and moderated in-person sessions. Rebuilt the first Maze test after prototype modification invalidated it — now lock all prototypes before launching live tests.
What this taught me

Usability testing is only as good as what you do with the data. A 71% success rate on Create Event could be read as acceptable — until you read the open feedback. Numbers and words together tell a different story than either one alone.

What Worked
The diverge-then-converge POV process. Having team members develop independent framings before converging opened genuinely different solution spaces. My interface-level framing and the team's systemic framing were both valid responses to the same data — and the synthesis was stronger than either alone.
What I'd Change
We should have tested the value proposition earlier. Multiple participants didn't understand what CalCal did differently from Google Calendar. That's a framing problem that usability testing revealed but couldn't fix — it needed to be addressed in onboarding before a single task was attempted.
Next Steps
Redesign Create Event as a stepped flow. Surface Find Better Time earlier in the event creation process — it's the app's most distinctive feature and was completely hidden. Add an onboarding moment that establishes the international-first value proposition before the home screen.

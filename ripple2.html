01 Overview
Context
Fig Build 2026- A 4-day remote hackathon sprint (~48 working hours). Ripple is an iOS mobile app designed to help people with chronic illness, disability, and fluctuating energy conditions navigate social planning with honesty and ease — without requiring medical explanation every time. The app uses a combination of text-based states and a visually expressive aura to communicate availability as an ambient, low-effort signal.
My Role
Led UX research, synthesis, and overall product direction. Responsible for translating early ideas into a cohesive interaction model and final prototype. Defined the core concept (aura-based energy system), established design principles, designed the full multi-state UI and "Soft Morning" design system, built the Figma prototype, and shaped and delivered the final presentation. Teammates contributed to early ideation, select research interviews, and feedback during wireframing.
Methods
Semi-structured interviews · Affinity mapping · Thematic analysis · Competitive analysis · Heuristic evaluation · Literature review (allostasis research) · Persona development · Accessibility audit

Competing against 650+ submissions from teams of 2–4, including professionals and graduate students. 02 The Problem

Most social and planning tools are built on a quiet assumption: that users have consistent, reliable energy. For people managing chronic illness, neurodivergence, or fluctuating conditions — POTS, ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, ADHD — that assumption breaks down, and the tools do nothing to address what comes next.

The result is a recurring, exhausting dilemma. Explaining capacity requires emotional labor and vulnerability. Masking energy leads to burnout. Cancelling plans carries guilt. Withdrawing quietly reduces social connection over time. Every interaction forces the same impossible choice between oversharing, masking, or disconnecting — none of which support authentic, sustainable relationships.

Core Problem Statement

How might we help people communicate social availability honestly and non-judgmentally — without requiring them to explain their condition every time?

03 Research & Discovery

Given the compressed timeline, research prioritized depth over breadth. I conducted 4 interviews personally; 3 additional interviews were supported by teammates — 7 total, with participants recruited through my personal network of people living with energy- or activity-limiting conditions.

Findings were synthesized in a live FigJam affinity mapping session, organized around four clusters: Energy Unpredictability, Social Pressure, Communication Barriers, and Emotional Impact (guilt, obligation, withdrawal). A lightweight literature review on allostasis ran in parallel, grounding the concept of energy as dynamic rather than a fixed, depleting resource.

Across interviews, participants consistently reported difficulty communicating availability even with close friends and partners — not just acquaintances. Guilt and fear of disappointing others were nearly universal. What participants needed wasn't a smarter scheduling tool — it was a way to signal capacity honestly, without over-explaining or masking their condition entirely.

A competitive analysis of existing apps — social planning tools, health trackers, accessibility-focused products — confirmed the gap. None addressed ambient, low-effort communication of energy state. Insights immediately informed design principles and the interaction model before any screens were drawn.

Key Research Finding

Difficulty communicating availability wasn't limited to casual relationships — it showed up even with close friends and partners. Guilt and fear of disappointment were the emotional undercurrent in nearly every interview. The design opportunity wasn't a new feature — it was a new framework: one that makes honest communication the path of least resistance.

04 Process
1
Research Sprint
7 interviews synthesized in a live affinity mapping session. Organized around four behavioral clusters before any sketching. Literature review of allostasis research ran in parallel to ground the energy metaphor scientifically. Insights immediately informed design principles and interaction model.
2
Principles Before Screens
Established 4 design principles before wireframing: low cognitive load, emotionally honest communication, dignity over productivity, inclusive by default. Principles acted as decision filters throughout the sprint — preventing rework and keeping every design decision grounded.
3
Design System First
Built the "Soft Morning" design system — palette, typography, spacing, motion, and illustration principles — before individual screens. Visual language aligned with the emotional tone of the product before a single UI screen was designed, ensuring consistency and enabling rapid prototyping without losing intent.
4
Hi-Fi First + AI-Assisted Prototyping
Designed high-fidelity wireframes before prototyping, then used Figma Make (AI prototyping tool) to translate them into an interactive prototype — defining the design system in prompts, building screens sequentially, correcting misalignments at each step, and adding animations last. 4–5 participants then explored the prototype in a lightweight usability testing session, informing minor refinements.

The Challenge
The aura visualization is visually expressive and emotionally resonant — but an animated ambient glow is completely inaccessible for screen reader users. Ripple's target population includes people who rely on VoiceOver and other assistive technology at low-energy moments, specifically when the app is most needed.
The Decision
The aura was designed as a visual layer over a text-based energy state system. The primary data is always text: "full energy," "taking it easy," "offline today." The aura enhances the experience — it doesn't carry it. Screen readers read the text state. The visual system earns its place on top of a working accessible foundation.
Accessibility-first meant the visual system had to earn its place on top of a working text foundation — not replace it.
05 Key Design Decisions
1
Designing for Fluctuating Energy
Energy is expressed as a flexible, shared ambient signal rather than a binary availability toggle. This supports honest communication without overcommitment — the goal is coordination around emotional safety, not productivity.
2
Energy Scale Tradeoff
Explored numeric (1–5) vs. simplified (high/medium/low) models. Decision deferred to future testing. Numeric is precise but potentially judgment-heavy; simplified is intuitive but less granular. Both approaches have merit depending on user context and condition type.
3
Accessibility First
The aura is a visual layer; text-based energy states are the source of truth. No reliance on animation or color alone. The visual system enhances the experience — it doesn't carry it. This ensures clarity, screen reader compatibility, and usability in low-energy moments when assistive technology is most needed.
4
Low-Energy as a First-Class Experience
Instead of treating low energy as a degraded state, I designed it intentionally: simplified interface, reduced actions, visual softening, notifications removed. The goal is not to encourage action — but to remove pressure. The screen communicates that rest is acceptable.
5
Reducing Emotional Friction
Soft, non-committal language throughout the interface reduces guilt and social pressure. The design supports self-care and preserves dignity in social interaction — coordination is framed around emotional safety, not obligation.
6
Future Wearable Insights (Exploratory)
Potential integration with wearable sensors — muscle tension, breathing, sweat — to track energy passively and reduce check-in friction further. Out of scope for the hackathon, but this informs Ripple's long-term vision and extends the ambient communication model.
06 Final Design

The final prototype centers on the home screen aura — a soft animated glow that reflects the user's current energy state. The aura isn't decorative; it's the primary communication surface. When a user checks in, the aura shifts in intensity, color, and rhythm to reflect their state across all their connections simultaneously, with no message required.

One of the most intentional screens in the prototype is the low-energy home state. When a user checks in at low capacity, the interface softens: the aura dims, quick-action tiles reduce to a single "Rest Mode" option, and notification badges disappear. The screen communicates that rest is acceptable — no prompts to improve, no red alerts, no productivity pressure. This is a first-class design decision, not a degraded fallback.

07 Outcomes
Fully interactive prototype delivered within the hackathon timeframe — complete research foundation, multi-state UI, "Soft Morning" design system, and competition-ready presentation deck, all under significant time and resource constraints.
Demonstrated a clear market gap — competitive analysis of 5+ existing tools confirmed no existing app addressed ambient, low-effort energy communication for this population.
Accessibility-first architecture — text-based energy states confirmed functional with screen reader before any visual aura layer was applied. WCAG 2.1 AA met throughout; key elements reach AAA.
Submitted in a hackathon with 650+ entries from teams of 2–4, including professionals and graduate students. Ripple is continuing as a Spring 2026 portfolio piece with extended research and a formal usability testing phase in planning.
What this taught me

Designing for a community you're personally part of is both an advantage and a risk. Being personally connected to the problem required discipline to separate my own assumptions from what participants actually said — and that discipline made the research more honest and the design more grounded.

What Worked
Establishing principles before wireframing prevented rework and kept every design decision grounded. "Inclusive by default" and "emotionally honest" acted as reliable filters throughout the sprint. The aura metaphor came directly from research, not from aesthetics — that grounding made it defensible. High-fidelity wireframes combined with AI-assisted prototyping enabled efficient iteration under time constraints.
What I'd Change
With more time I'd test the energy scale more rigorously — 3 states vs. 5 vs. a continuous slider. The decision to simplify was informed by cognitive load feedback but not formally validated. I'd also want to test the soft, non-committal language in the Plan Together screen with users outside the chronic illness community to understand how the framing lands.
Next Steps
Test numeric vs. simplified energy representation models. Expand research to 15+ participants with broader condition representation. Explore the notification system — how does Ripple communicate a friend's energy change without creating anxiety? Conduct formal accessibility testing with screen reader users. Investigate wearable integration for passive energy tracking.

01 Overview
Context
Bondfire is a native Slack application designed to help teams build authentic connections without disrupting their workflow. It embeds lightweight, interactive experiences directly into Slack — eliminating the need for scheduled meetings or external tools. Built as a real product with a co-founder and software engineer.
My Role
I shaped the product direction, research foundation, and UX strategy. My contributions spanned market research, feature ideation, interaction design within Slack Block Kit constraints, and co-creating the pitch that took Bondfire to a competitive business pitch final.
Methods
Business & market research · Competitive analysis · User interviews · Survey research · Interaction design · Slack Block Kit prototyping · Usability feedback · Pitch development & presentation

02 The Problem

Remote work created a specific kind of loneliness — the kind that exists alongside constant communication. You're in meetings all day, on Slack all day, and still don't know anything real about the people you work with.

Existing team-building solutions compounded the problem. They required leaving the workflow entirely, were often built around competition in industries already exhausted by it, and gave leadership nothing measurable — no way to understand what was actually happening with team health or justify the time spent.

The deeper design assumption underneath all of it: that team bonding tools are for extroverts. People who enjoy being put on the spot, who thrive in group settings, who don't find "mandatory fun" draining. The quieter people on the team — the ones who wanted to connect but not perform — had no tool built for them.

High friction
Most tools require leaving the workflow entirely to participate
Low inclusion
Activity formats tend to reward extroverted participation styles
No visibility
Leadership has little insight into team dynamics or engagement health
Core Design Challenge

How might we help distributed teams build authentic connections inside their existing workflow — without disrupting deep work, rewarding only extroverts, or giving leadership nothing to measure?

03 Research & Discovery

Research started with market and competitive analysis — looking at what tools already existed, where participation dropped off, and what assumptions those tools were making about users. That grounding came before any feature ideas were on the table.

I conducted interviews with team leaders and managers and surveyed remote workers about connection and engagement. The goal wasn't just to confirm the problem — it was to understand the specific tensions different people were navigating, so the design could work for all of them.

Three personas emerged that were not interchangeable: Sarah (People Ops Director, needs measurable ROI and culture data), Marcus (Engineering Team Lead, needs his team to trust each other fast), and Alex (Software Engineer, wants organic connection without forced participation). Most existing tools were designed for Sarah and Marcus — at the expense of Alex. And Alex's disengagement undermined everything the others were trying to build.

The design had to work for all three at once. That constraint became the creative engine for every decision that followed.

Sarah · People Ops Director · Primary Persona
"I know team bonding is important, but I need to prove it's working."
Leadership needed evidence, not entertainment. An analytics layer wasn't a nice-to-have — it was the feature that made budget justification possible and differentiated Bondfire from competitors.
Alex · Software Engineer · Key Persona
"I want to connect with my team, but I don't want another mandatory meeting."
Alex was the hardest user to design for and the most important. If the introverted, deep-work person opts out, participation collapses and the product fails for everyone. Designing for Alex first made the whole system stronger.
Key Research Finding

Existing tools designed for the most willing participants and called it success. Dropout came from everyone else. Bondfire had to earn participation from the people least inclined to give it — or the engagement would never be real.

04 Process
1
Research & Principles
Market and competitive research, user interviews, and surveys grounded the product direction before any features were decided. Four design principles emerged from this work: Slack-Native First, Participation Over Performance, Respect for Real Work, and Strategic Intelligence.
2
Feature Ideation & Adaptation
Contributed to game design concepts and helped adapt real-world and Zoom-based activities into Slack-compatible formats. Not every activity translates — constraints in Slack Block Kit shaped which ideas were worth pursuing and which would create more friction than connection.
3
UX Within Constraints
Improved readability and usability within Slack Block Kit's limitations — introducing clearer content hierarchy, spacing and formatting improvements, and step-by-step flow clarity. Advocated for small UX changes that meaningfully improved participation and usability without requiring engineering overhauls.
4
Pitch & Communication
Co-created and delivered the final product pitch for the 4CD Business Pitch Competition. Translated the product vision and research foundation into a clear, compelling narrative — positioning Bondfire as both a team tool and a business solution for People Ops.
Information architecture — entry points through post-activity analytics

Bondfire's information architecture — from game initiation through completion, with analytics captured throughout. All gameplay stays inside Slack threads.

The Challenge
Early game launch flows had too many configuration steps — team leaders had to manually select participants, choose settings, and preview before launching. Feedback showed it felt overwhelming and added enough friction that some leaders simply didn't start games. Complexity at the entry point was killing participation before it began.
The Decision
One-step launch with smart defaults. Auto-includes all channel members. Advanced options are accessible but collapsed. The goal was to make the default path feel effortless — and trust that leaders who wanted control could find it without forcing it on everyone.
The best interface is one that gets out of the way. Simplicity wasn't a compromise — it was the principle that made the product actually usable.
05 Final Design

The product covers multiple activity types designed for different team sizes, energy levels, and personality styles — so no single working style gets left out. All gameplay happens inside Slack, keeping the experience native and low-friction. Points are earned for participation, not just winning. Leader controls are always accessible, respecting that work comes first.

One of the most important design decisions was treating the analytics layer as a first-class feature from the start, not an afterthought. Leadership needs more than a fun product — they need something they can point to. Giving People Ops visibility into team engagement patterns made Bondfire defensible as a business investment, not just a culture perk.

06 Outcomes
4CD Business Pitch Finalist (2025) — research insights and product strategy presented to judges and stakeholders in a competitive pitch environment. Recognized for the product's practicality and dual value for teams and leadership.
Strong positive feedback from industry professionals — specifically around how Bondfire works within Slack rather than outside it, and how the analytics layer bridges the gap between team experience and business value.
Active product in ongoing development — plans to expand features, deepen analytics, and explore AI-powered personalization. The pitch was a milestone, not an endpoint.
Validated the dual-user design strategy — designing for both the employee experience and the leadership use case simultaneously. Feedback confirmed that both audiences found the product relevant to their actual needs.
Learned what doesn't translate — not every in-person or Zoom activity works natively in Slack. The constraint of Block Kit forced clearer thinking about what "low-friction connection" actually means in practice.
What this taught me

Designing for three stakeholders with genuinely different goals forces clarity. Every decision had to account for Alex, Marcus, and Sarah at the same time. That constraint produced better design than optimizing for any single persona would have.

What Worked
"Participation over performance" as a design principle, not just a value statement. It resolved dozens of decisions without requiring a separate debate each time — game mechanics, activity formats, how points work. Principles that do real work are worth more than principles that sound good.
What I'd Change
I would have engaged the leadership persona (Sarah, People Ops) earlier in the process. The analytics layer came later than it should have — and it became clear mid-project that it was the feature that justified the business conversation, not a nice addition. The research insight was there. The prioritization came too late.
Next Steps
AI-powered personalization — activity recommendations based on team patterns and timing. Expand beyond Slack to Microsoft Teams. Build a custom activity creator for enterprise accounts. Deepen the analytics with predictive engagement indicators and benchmarking tools.

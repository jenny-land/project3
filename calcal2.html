02 The Problem

Most calendar apps are designed around a simple assumption: the user exists in one time zone. That assumption breaks quickly in real life — the moment your team, your family, or your social life crosses a border, users are left manually calculating time differences, relying on external tools, and still making errors.

Three core problems emerged from research: time zone confusion (users calculating differences themselves), misaligned reminders (notifications tied to the user's time, not the event's context), and fragmented workflows (multiple tools increasing cognitive load). But the deeper issue wasn't just logistical.

Missing a meeting is inconvenient. Missing a moment with someone you care about because the app didn't account for the difference — that's a human problem. That distinction shaped every decision we made.

Primary HMW — Authored by Jennifer, Adopted by Team

How might we create a calendar app that seamlessly adapts to multiple time zones and integrates with existing tools — helping busy professionals and students manage international schedules without manual calculations, workflow interruptions, or miscommunication?

03 Research & Discovery

The team conducted surveys and semi-structured interviews with 9 participants — a mix of college students and working professionals between ages 18 and 54. Research targeted people managing international relationships or schedules alongside students balancing unpredictable, multi-context commitments.

I led participant recruiting and facilitated moderated in-person sessions alongside Maze's unmoderated remote testing. The combination gave us both the depth of observed behavior and the breadth of quantitative task metrics — neither alone would have been enough.

Findings were synthesized through affinity mapping into four themes: Reminder Preferences, Timezone Management, Integration Needs, and Complex Schedules. A competitive analysis covering Google Calendar (55.6% of participants' primary tool), Apple Calendar, Re:Catch, and Momentum confirmed the gap: none offered native timezone conflict detection. Users were solving the problem themselves — with workarounds.

Secondary research validated our findings. A published case study on Google Calendar's timezone handling documented nearly identical friction points — confirming we weren't designing for edge cases.

Participant Feedback · Usability Test
"Overall layout is confusing. What does this app solve? Why is it better than a regular calendar app?"
Multiple participants couldn't articulate CalCal's value proposition — revealing a gap between the features we designed and how we surfaced their purpose. The app needed onboarding before the home screen.
Participant Feedback · Create Event Flow
"Too many questions to create an event. Looked cluttered and boring — it just felt like work."
Task completion rates don't capture experience quality. Users completed the Create Event flow at 71.4% — but described it as frustrating. That gap between completion and satisfaction is where retention is lost.
Key Research Finding

Task success rates are an incomplete quality signal. CalCal's participants completed flows at acceptable rates while simultaneously describing the experience as cluttered and frustrating — a retention risk the numbers alone would never have revealed. This is what mixed-methods testing makes visible.

04 Process
1
Discovery & Framing
Interviews, surveys, competitive analysis, and secondary research. Team members developed independent POV statements before converging — my HMW formulation was adopted verbatim as the team's primary framing, and became the compass for every design decision that followed.
2
Flows & Architecture
I designed all three task flows and user flows: Create Event (with timezone conflict detection branch), Sync Calendar, and Set Time Zone. These flows shaped the prototype structure and became the direct basis for all three Maze usability test tasks.
3
Prototype & Leadership
The team built wireframes across all core screens into an interactive Figma prototype. This project required significant hands-on leadership — I coordinated weekly sessions, organized additional working time to meet deadlines, and adapted how I communicated to support different team needs.
4
Test & Synthesize
Unmoderated Maze testing plus moderated local sessions. I led recruiting and facilitated in-person sessions. When the test environment was compromised mid-test, I rebuilt the prototype and recreated the test in a controlled environment — ensuring we were working from clean, reliable data.
Task flows — Create Event, Sync Calendar, Set Time Zone

Three task flows mapped in Figma. The Create Event flow includes a timezone conflict detection branch — the most complex path and the hardest usability test task.

The Problem
The Create Event form tried to do everything on one screen: event name, all-day toggle, start and end times with timezone display, recurrence, attendees, reminders, and conflict warnings. Usability testing showed the highest friction of any flow — 71.4% success, 71.1% misclick rate, and feedback describing it as "cluttered" and "just work."
The Recommendation
Split the form into progressive steps: Basic Info → Reminders → Attendees → Confirm. This pattern reduces cognitive load and creates natural checkpoints — giving the conflict detection warning room to live without overwhelming the primary task.
The most valuable feature — timezone conflict detection — was also the hardest to find. Good research reveals not just what's broken, but what's buried.
05 Final Design

The prototype addressed all three research-validated problem areas: a timezone settings flow that lets users toggle between device timezone and a searchable manual zone, a reminders overlay with stacked notifications and a persistent reminder option, and a calendar sync screen supporting OAuth connection to external services.

The highest-performing screen in usability testing was the reminders overlay — 92.9% task success, intuitive enough that participants rarely misclicked. The lowest was the Find Better Time feature — 0 direct successes, entirely reliant on indirect paths. The gap between those two numbers is itself a finding: discoverability is a design problem, not a user problem.

06 Outcomes & Next Steps
Reminders overlay: 92.9% task success — the best-performing flow in the prototype. Low drop-off, low misclick rate. Participants described it as intuitive and well-designed without prompting.
HMW question adopted verbatim by the team — written during the diverge-then-converge POV session, it survived peer review unchanged and became the compass for every design decision that followed.
Settings-based flows (Set Time Zone, Sync Calendar) both achieved 86.7% task success — above target. Learnable paths with acceptable misclick rates, confirming the settings architecture was sound even if the entry points weren't always obvious.
Find Better Time: 0 direct successes across all test participants. All completions were indirect — a critical insight. The app's most distinctive feature was entirely undiscoverable, and would have shipped invisible without this data.
Led recruiting and rebuilt the test environment mid-study after the prototype was modified post-launch. Confirmed with the instructor and continued forward with clean, reliable data. Now lock all prototypes before launching live tests.
Looking back

CalCal was my first full UX project — and a turning point. It's where I started to understand that good design doesn't just organize time. It supports how people show up in their lives. The final product isn't the version I would design today, but the thinking behind it still holds.

Redesign Event Creation
Split the Create Event form into a stepped flow — Basic Info, Reminders, Attendees, Confirm. The single-screen approach created unnecessary cognitive load and buried the conflict detection feature that made CalCal worth using.
Surface the Core Feature
Find Better Time had 0 direct successes — not because it wasn't useful, but because no one could find it. Move it earlier in the event creation flow, where the context makes its purpose immediately clear.
Add Onboarding First
Multiple participants asked "why is this better than Google Calendar?" — which means the value proposition wasn't landing before they started using it. An onboarding moment that establishes the international-first premise needs to come before the home screen.
